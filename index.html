Welcome to Open Power Quality

Open source hardware, software, and data for low cost, crowdsourced power quality monitoring and analysis

Low cost, open source hardware

We can build an OPQ device for less than US$75, and the schematics are available if you want to do it yourself.

We want to create and support an ecosystem of hardware developers that accelerate the development of new designs to satisfy different needs.

Our initial design is based upon a Raspberry PI. We plan to decrease the cost and size, and increase the functionality through a more custom design in future. Come help us invent the future of power quality monitoring!

Cloud-based, open source software service

OPQ hardware devices communicate power quality events to a cloud-based software service using WiFi.

For consumers, this means that you can determine if a power quality problem is local to your own house or is more widespread in your neighborhood or city.

For organizations, this means you can deploy OPQ hardware devices throughout your facility, and get an aggregate view of power quality.

The OPQ software service can be installed and run locally, or deployed to a hosting environment such as CloudBees or Heroku

Analytics

Uploading the raw data is only the start. The real power of OPQ is the kinds of analytics it makes possible.

First, each power quality monitoring device is associated with a user-supplied location. To support privacy, users can "coarsen" their location information up to 1 square mile. Coarsened location data still enables the service to provide useful community statistics without revealing the precise individual location of the monitoring devices.

Second, the community nature of the data makes it trivial to determine if a power quality event is local to you or more widespread on the grid.

Third, correlation of power quality data with other forms of information can provide valuable insight into the impact of renewable energy. For example, are power quality events more common in areas with high concentration of rooftop solar? Do power quality events correlate with weather data---for example, a rapidly moving cloud cover?

Our software architecture is designed to support "plugin" analytics. Have an idea for a new analytic? Let us know!
